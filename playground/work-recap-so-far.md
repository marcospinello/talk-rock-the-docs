⏺ Here are three narrative spines that fit "Writers are bandmates, not session musicians" for a 40–45 min slot to a tech-writing audience.

  Spine A — Anatomy of a band (role-driven) ⭐ recommended

  Walk through the roles inside a working band, and for each one map the docs-team analogue. Each section follows the same beat: the role → a real-band case study
   → what session-musician thinking gets wrong → a Monday-morning move.

  Likely sections: rhythm section (style guides, IA, linters — invisible until missing), lead instruments (voice, narrative, the examples that get screenshotted
  on Twitter), frontperson (DevRel, the public face), producer/engineer (review, dogfooding, mixing), the manager (doc team lead, advocacy, scheduling), roadies &
   venue (tooling, platform, CI). Open with the session-musician problem, close with what changes when the writer joins the band.

  Why I'd pick this: roles are sticky, the audience recognises them, and the structure gives you natural slots for case studies + tactics + the occasional
  musician memoir without forcing it. Easy to time-box (each role ≈ 4–5 min). Risk: gets list-y if every section follows the exact same beat — needs one or two
  sections that break the pattern.

  Spine B — From rehearsal to gig (lifecycle-driven)

  Trace a band's lifecycle — forming, rehearsing, recording, soundcheck, gig, tour — and map each phase onto a phase of docs work. The
  bandmate-vs-session-musician argument shows up inside each phase as the wrong way vs the right way.

  Why I'd pass: lifecycle talks read like waterfall lectures unless you fight them hard. Tech writers have heard a lot of "first you plan, then you draft, then
  you publish" talks. The phases also don't map cleanly to docs work — soundcheck = pre-publish review? touring = maintenance? — so you spend energy defending the
   metaphor instead of using it.

  Spine C — Three myths (argument-driven)

  Open by naming three "session musician truisms" the audience has heard — e.g. writers come in at the end, writers don't need to be in design reviews, writers
  can be a shared service across teams. Bust each with a real-band counter-example + a tactic. Close on the bandmate alternative.

  Why I'd pass for now: myth-busting talks live or die on whether all three myths are equally sharp and equally wrong. If one is weaker, the talk sags in the
  middle. It's also more confrontational — great if the room needs waking up, but you said it's mostly fellow writers, so you'd be busting myths everyone in the
  room already disagrees with.

  ---
  My recommendation: Spine A. It uses all three of your raw-material types naturally (case studies per role, musician memoir as colour, tactics as the takeaway),
  it's easy to time, and "anatomy of a band" gives the deck a visual through-line you can lean on with the existing themes.
