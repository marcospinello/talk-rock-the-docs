## Title

**Rock the docs: Writers are bandmates, not session musicians**

---

## Abstract

Most docs teams operate like session musicians: nail the take, hand off the file, leave the studio, never hear the record. I want to argue for a different posture — writers as **bandmates**, in the room until the work is done.

I spent years in an 8-piece disco band that became an instrumental power trio — the same kind of contraction many docs teams have lived through, by design or reorg. I co-mixed and co-mastered the album that came out the other side of that change. Four things turned out to be load-bearing in a band, and load-bearing in a docs practice for the same reasons: the rhythm section nobody applauds (foundation), the voice nobody owns alone, the producer's chair nobody likes sitting in (polish), and the rig that quietly decides what you can sound like (tooling).

Stories from my band — including tracks from our album *Be-Style* — anchor each section, but the takeaways are tactical, not metaphorical.

You'll leave with:

- A four-part lens — Foundation, Voice, Polish, Tooling — for diagnosing what your docs practice is missing.
- An argument, and the language for it, for treating voice as a band sport rather than a writer's solo.
- A case for staying past "doc shipped," and what that looks like in practice.
- A reframe of CI and tooling as instruments, not infrastructure.
- One concrete foundation thing to put on next week's calendar.

---

## Who is this talk for?

The talk is built for the mixed audience Write the Docs draws.

For **technical writers** — solo or in teams — it offers a frame and a vocabulary for pushing back on the session-musician roles many of us have quietly accepted: the writer who takes the brief, ships the draft, and is on the next ticket before the doc finds out whether it works. The bandmate posture is a way of asking for, and earning, a seat past the handoff.

For **developers and support staff** who write or shape docs, the talk reframes voice and polish as collaborative work, not a writer's solo. It hands non-writers a vocabulary to participate in voice, mixing, and review without feeling they're stepping on someone's craft.

For **managers of docs teams**, the talk is a diagnostic. Most "our docs are broken" conversations turn out to be one of four conversations in disguise — at the foundation, at voice, at polish, or in tooling — and the band frame makes the failure mode visible.

For **community contributors and enthusiasts** without "writer" in the job title, the talk legitimises a posture they often already have: caring about the doc past publish, treating review as iteration, asking why the workflow sounds the way it does.

The talk is deliberately not tool-prescriptive. It mentions doc-as-code, CI, and linters, but the argument applies whether your team is on Markdown in Git, a structured content management system, or anything between. The point isn't which tools you use; it's whether the people using them think of themselves as session musicians or as bandmates.

## Why is this helpful, applicable, important?

The talk delivers four sections, each built on the same shape: **band story → docs analogy → the misinterpretation the session-musician mindset makes → one tactical takeaway.** The audience hears that rhythm five times so the frame sticks.

**Specific examples and case studies.** The 8-piece-to-trio shrinkage is a real career arc, not a metaphor I plucked from the air. The talk uses two tracks from my band's album *Be-Style* as case studies: "Empire State Building" (track 4) anchors the foundation section — three players, one pulse, one building — and "First Born" (track 2) anchors the voice section, where a power trio's three instruments do the work of a singer. The audio is optional (~30 seconds per cue, where the venue permits); the talk works without sound.

**Workflows examined in depth.** The "Polish" section is the talk's emotional center. I co-mixed and co-mastered the band's record, sitting next to the engineer for hours past the point of "tracked." The talk argues the docs equivalent is staying in the room past "doc shipped," and dissects review-as-checkbox as the session-musician handoff in disguise — including the specific cultural pushback most teams face ("we already shipped, move on") and how to reframe it without sounding precious.

**Key takeaways, with specifics.**

- *Foundation:* pick one deferred foundation thing — style guide, IA review, linter setup — and put it on next week's calendar. One thing.
- *Voice:* stop treating voice as the writer's deliverable. Run a voice review with a developer and a support engineer in the room.
- *Polish:* name and refuse the "doc is delivered, and then?" handoff pattern.
- *Tooling:* ask of every CI step, "what does this let our docs sound like?" If you can't answer, it's probably the wrong step.

**Anything else.** I built the deck doc-as-code in Marp and open-sourced it on GitHub under CC BY 4.0. The talk and its artifact are the same thing — a small embodiment of the argument the talk is making.

## Other Information

I'm a senior technical writer at Booking.com and the guitarist in an instrumental power trio. The "co-mixed and co-mastered the album" line on my CV is the entire reason I trust this metaphor: I've actually been in the producer's chair, not just heard one described. I have no computer-science background — I came to docs from the curious-as-a-cat side of the room, which I think makes me a better translator between writers and engineers, not a worse one.

The deck and source for this talk live on GitHub under CC BY 4.0, partly so the talk doubles as a doc-as-code reference and partly because the argument the talk makes — bandmate, not session musician — is hard to credibly make from a closed deck. The band's album *Be-Style* is on Bandcamp at spinello-band.bandcamp.com.

This would be the talk's first outing at a conference.
